Core Training Module

Our AI Ethics Framework

Why This Training Matters

As a fintech company, we leverage artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) to deliver fast, efficient financial services. Our AI systems process loan applications, assess creditworthiness, detect fraud, and make other high-stakes decisions affecting millions of customers.

With this power comes significant responsibility. AI systems can perpetuate or amplify existing biases, leading to discriminatory outcomes, regulatory penalties, and reputational damage. Recent enforcement actions have resulted in fines exceeding $100 million for companies that deployed biased AI systems.

Your role in maintaining ethical AI practices is critical‚Äîregardless of your position. Whether you're in engineering, product, operations, customer service, or any other function, you have a responsibility to recognize and escalate ethical concerns.

The FAT Framework: Your Daily Decision-Making Tool

Our company uses the FAT framework to guide all AI development and deployment decisions. FAT stands for Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency. You should apply these principles whenever you interact with or make decisions about AI systems:

‚öñÔ∏è

Fairness

Definition: AI systems must produce equitable outcomes and must not systematically disadvantage any demographic group.

In your work: If you notice approval rates, fraud flags, or other outcomes varying significantly by protected characteristics (race, gender, age, national origin), you must escalate immediately. Statistical disparities require investigation‚Äîthey may indicate illegal discrimination.

Key metric: Disparate impact ratios above 1.25:1 trigger mandatory review under CFPB guidelines.

üë§

Accountability

Definition: Clear human ownership and responsibility for all AI system decisions and outcomes.

In your work: Every AI-driven decision must have a designated owner who can review, explain, and if necessary, override that decision. You cannot defer to "the algorithm" when customers or regulators request explanations. Document your decision-making process.

Escalation path: Your Manager ‚Üí Ethics Committee ‚Üí Chief Risk Officer ‚Üí Legal/Compliance

üîç

Transparency

Definition: AI decision-making processes must be explainable to customers, regulators, and internal stakeholders.

In your work: You must be able to provide customers with specific reasons for adverse actions (denials, limit decreases, etc.). "Our AI system declined your application" is legally insufficient and violates ECOA requirements. Use our explainability dashboard to generate compliant adverse action notices.

Legal requirement: Regulation B requires specific reasons within 30 days of adverse action.

Regulatory Landscape: What You Must Know

U.S. Federal Requirements

Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) / Regulation B:

  • Adverse action notices required: Must provide specific, meaningful reasons for credit denials
  • Prohibited basis: Cannot discriminate on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, or receipt of public assistance
  • Disparate impact standard: Even unintentional discrimination is illegal if it disproportionately harms protected groups
  • Penalties: Actual damages, punitive damages up to $10,000 per violation, attorney fees

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) Guidance:

  • UDAAP provisions: AI practices must not be unfair, deceptive, or abusive
  • Model explainability: "Black box" defenses are explicitly rejected‚Äîcompanies must explain AI decisions
  • Regular testing required: Must conduct ongoing bias testing and monitoring
  • Third-party vendors: Liable for vendor AI systems‚Äîcannot outsource compliance

European Union AI Act (2024)

Applies if we serve EU customers or use EU data:

  • Risk classification: Financial services AI is "high-risk" requiring enhanced safeguards
  • Mandatory requirements: Risk management system, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency, human oversight
  • Conformity assessment: High-risk systems require third-party audits before deployment
  • Penalties: Up to ‚Ç¨35 million or 7% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher
  • Right to explanation: Customers have legal right to meaningful information about AI decisions

Result: CFPB enforcement action, required system replacement, $25 million in restitution to denied applicants.

Lesson: Regulatory compliance requires explainability from day one‚Äîretrofit is expensive and may be impossible.

Result: Class action lawsuit, $85 million settlement, mandatory bias testing requirements.

Lesson: Training data that reflects past human bias will produce biased AI. "That's what the data shows" is not a legal defense.

Suspected Bias in Loan Approvals

Your Role: Product Manager, Lending Products

You manage our personal loan approval system, which uses an ML model trained on five years of historical loan performance data. The system has been in production for six months and processes approximately 10,000 applications per month.

The Situation

Your data analyst runs a routine disparate impact analysis and flags a concerning pattern: applicants from zip codes with majority Black and Hispanic populations are being denied at twice the rate (40% denial) compared to similar-credit-profile applicants from other zip codes (20% denial).

Upon investigation, you discover:

  • The model uses zip code as one of 47 input features
  • Historically, these zip codes had higher default rates, but this reflects decades of discriminatory lending practices (redlining) rather than individual creditworthiness
  • When you control for credit score, income, and DTI (Debt-to-Income) ratio, applicants from these zip codes actually have slightly better repayment rates
  • The disparate impact ratio is 2:1 (well above the 1.25:1 threshold requiring investigation)
  • Your system has processed approximately 60,000 applications since launch

Stakeholder Positions:

  • CEO: "The system is hitting our target approval rates and loss forecasts. Any changes will impact our quarterly numbers."
  • Chief Risk Officer: "We need to investigate this immediately. Continuing operation could expose us to significant regulatory liability."
  • Chief Data Officer: "We can retrain the model without zip code and have a new version ready in three weeks."
  • Legal: "Once we have knowledge of potential discrimination, every subsequent denial using this system creates additional liability."

Disparate Impact Liability: A 2:1 denial ratio far exceeds the 1.25:1 threshold triggering prima facie discrimination under CFPB guidance.
